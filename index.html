New York Jets 2026 NFL Draft — Pros and Cons of Key Picks

The Jets’ 2026 draft class was strong, with three first-rounders and several later picks that could help the rebuild. Here’s a breakdown of the main picks and their trade-offs.

1. David Bailey, DE (No. 2, Texas Tech)

Pros

  • Led FBS in sacks (14.5) and quarterback pressure rate (20.4%) in 2025.
  • 4.5-second 40-yard dash; elite get-off to burn edges.
  • Fits a pass rush that was a decade without a double-digit sack threat.

Cons

  • Limited run defense (only 8 run stops in 2025).
  • Some analysts felt Arvell Reese (Ohio State) had a higher ceiling.

2. Kenyon Sadiq, TE (No. 16, Oregon)

Pros

  • Rare physical traits for TE — 4.39 40, 43.5" vertical, 241 lbs.
  • Can box out and outrun defenders.
  • Adds explosiveness to a scoring-challenged offense.

Cons

  • No direct need for TE; may be used more as a flex receiver than a traditional tight end.

3. Omar Cooper Jr., WR (No. 30, Indiana)

Pros

  • Mendoza’s top target in 2025.
  • Adds another playmaker to Wilson’s WR group.
  • Fits the Jets’ offensive scheme.

Cons

  • Going to be wide reciver two could be a wide reciver one on other teams.

4. D’Angelo Ponds, CB (No. 50, Indiana)

Pros

  • Fits coach Aaron Glenn’s scheme.
  • Undersized but can be a tone-setter on the back end.

Cons

  • Limited size and coverage range; may need to be used in specific schemes. Small build

5. Darrell Jackson Jr., DT (No. 103, Florida State)

Pros

  • Good value pick.
  • Adds depth to the defensive line.

Cons

  • Not a top-tier pass rusher; more of a depth piece.

6. Cade Klubnik, QB (No. 110, Clemson)

Pros

  • Quick release, solid rhythm when not pressured, dual-threat ability.
  • 40+ college starts; could be a dynamic floor for Geno Smith.

Cons

  • Decision-making questions, especially under pressure.
  • 2025 regression hurt stock; no QB need at the time.

7. Anez Cooper, G (No. 188, Miami)

Pros

  • Adds offensive line depth.
  • Good value for a late-round pick.

Cons

  • Not a top-tier starter; more of a depth piece.Could be a starter if injuries happen on the offensive Line.

8. VJ Payne, SAF (No. 228, Kansas State)

Pros

  • Adds depth to the secondary.
  • Fits the Jets’ scheme.

Cons

  • Limited size; more of a depth contributor.

Overall Take

The Jets maximized their draft capital, landing three immediate-impact starters (Bailey, Sadiq, Cooper) and several depth pieces. Bailey is the biggest upgrade, while Sadiq and Cooper add offensive firepower. Klubnik was a riskier fourth-round pick but could provide upside if he develops quickly.
